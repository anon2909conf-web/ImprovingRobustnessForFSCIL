Replace Augmentation:
A Novel Feature-Space Mixing Strategy for Few-Shot Class-Incremental Learning

Domain Computer Vision · Few-Shot Learning
Contribution Feature Augmentation Method
01

Abstract

Few-shot class-incremental learning (FSCIL) presents a fundamental challenge in continual learning: acquiring new knowledge from limited examples while preventing catastrophic forgetting of base classes. Traditional data augmentation methods often prove insufficient in the few-shot regime.

We introduce Replace Augmentation, a novel feature-space mixing strategy that operates at both spatial and frequency domains. Unlike conventional mixup techniques, our method strategically combines embeddings from different samples through:

  • Spatial mixing — Linear interpolation of feature embeddings
  • Frequency domain manipulation — FFT-based magnitude and phase mixing
  • Adaptive noise injection — Controlled perturbation for regularization

This approach generates diverse, synthetic training samples that enhance model generalization without requiring additional labeled data.

02

Methodology

Core Algorithm

Replace Augmentation operates on feature embeddings extracted from the encoder network:

1
Feature Extraction: Input samples pass through encoder to obtain feature representations
2
Spatial Mixing: For odd-indexed samples, create synthetic features via interpolation:
f'[i] = λ · f[i] + (1-λ) · f[j] where j is randomly selected
3
Frequency Transform: Apply FFT to decompose features into magnitude and phase:
F = FFT(f), |F| = magnitude, ∠F = phase
4
Frequency Mixing: Mix magnitude and phase components across random pairs
5
Noise Regularization: Add Gaussian noise (σ = 0.02) and normalize

Integration with MoCo Framework

Replace Augmentation seamlessly integrates into the Momentum Contrast (MoCo) framework, applying augmentation to both query and key encoders during contrastive learning.

Key Innovation

Unlike traditional augmentation that operates on raw pixels, Replace Augmentation works directly in the feature space, allowing semantically meaningful combinations. The frequency domain manipulation via FFT provides an additional dimension of diversity that pixel-space methods cannot achieve.

Key Findings

1
Frequency Domain Matters: Ablation studies show that FFT-based mixing contributes significantly (2-3% improvement) beyond spatial mixing alone.
2
Fine-Grained Benefits: Largest improvements on CUB-200 suggest the method is particularly effective for subtle feature discrimination.
3
Catastrophic Forgetting Reduced: Base class accuracy remains stable across incremental sessions, indicating better knowledge retention.
